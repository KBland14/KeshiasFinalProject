Paracord

Paracord was the spark that started my crafting journey. Also known as 550 cord, it’s a strong, versatile rope with countless uses. Many people rely on it as a survival tool for camping and outdoor activities, while others use it around the house for everything from securing items to improvising a clothesline. I took a different path and began creating paracord bracelets to sell, helping fund my younger sister’s school trip.

When I first started, I watched tutorials and assumed it would be simple. In some ways, I was right. The basic concept and braiding technique were straightforward. The real challenge was remembering the correct direction, patterns, and how to properly start each bracelet. Once I found my rhythm, everything clicked. I could easily complete five bracelets in an hour without any trouble.

As my confidence grew, I wanted to make my work more personalized. I began exploring the wide range of bracelet designs available, from simple patterns to increasingly complex weaves. While I didn’t master every style, I became skilled in several of them. My curiosity didn’t stop there. With more research, I discovered that designs could even be “sewn” into the bracelets, adding another layer of creativity and individuality. It’s a relaxing and enjoyable process, though it can be demanding on the hands. The rough texture of the cord can take a toll after a while, but the end result always makes it worth the effort.

LEGOs

I want to start by saying that LEGOs are not just for kids. While there have always been sets designed for older builders, they were often tied to movies or specific themes. More recently, LEGO has introduced builds that feel more creative and unexpected, and they are incredibly fun to work on. That said, I wouldn’t recommend these sets for younger children, as they contain a lot of very small pieces.

Fun doesn’t always mean easy, though. These builds can also be extremely frustrating. The pieces are tiny, and they don’t always stay in place while you’re adding new sections. I ran into this problem repeatedly, with parts falling off every time I tried to move forward.

While I briefly considered using super glue, I discovered something far better: LEGO glue. It works similarly to super glue, but you only need a tiny dab on part of the block for it to hold securely. The best part is that it’s completely reversible. By placing the LEGO pieces in warm water, the glue releases as if it were never there, allowing the pieces to separate without damage.

Diamond Art

Diamond Art and color-by-number are the most relaxing of my creative outlets. I know color-by-number isn’t traditionally considered a craft, but at times it feels like one. Diamond Art has grown in popularity, especially as new designs and formats continue to emerge. While it can be calming, it can also be challenging until the final piece is placed. Searching for the correct diamonds, keeping them from shifting out of place, and staying organized can feel overwhelming, yet the process remains deeply satisfying.

Color-by-number offers a similar sense of relaxation and fulfillment. At first glance, it may sound like a children’s activity, but there are many apps and books specifically designed for adults. I enjoy starting my day with a cup of tea while coloring the daily image, or winding down after a long shift by working on one in the evening. It helps clear my mind and creates a quiet moment of focus, whether I’m preparing for the day ahead or decompressing after work.

Like Diamond Art, color-by-number can also be stressful at times, especially when working on a more complex image and trying to track down the last section of a particular color. Unlike Diamond Art, there aren’t many simple ways to preserve finished color-by-number pieces beyond framing them. While other options exist, I haven’t taken the time to explore them fully. With Diamond Art, however, a sealing glue can be applied to secure the diamonds in place, allowing the finished piece to be displayed or framed without worry.

Wood Work

I’ll be honest—I haven’t done a lot with woodworking yet. So far, I’ve mostly painted pieces and sealed them with resin to keep the designs in place. I do plan to explore more, especially now that I’m getting an engraver. The wood pieces in my gallery have all been either painted or painted and resin-sealed. There are a few building projects I’d like to try, but those will have to wait until the summer.
